## What Makes Commercial Masonry Different

Commercial masonry work is not simply residential work at larger scale. The scope is larger, the stakes are higher, and the project requirements are different in ways that matter.

**Occupied buildings.** Most commercial masonry work happens on buildings that are in active use. Retail tenants, office workers, residents, or congregations don't pause operations for a masonry project. Staging, sequencing, noise management, and access coordination are part of the job.

**Documentation requirements.** Property managers and building owners often need written scopes, phase-based progress reports, and before/after photo documentation for ownership reporting, board presentations, or insurance purposes. That documentation is part of how we work — not an add-on.

**Scope accuracy.** Commercial buildings have more complexity: multiple elevations, parapet walls, penetrations, lintels at every opening, varying substrate conditions. Getting scope right up front requires an experienced eye and a thorough assessment. We don't estimate from the truck.

**Project management.** A commercial masonry project isn't a one-day job. Sequencing, material lead times, weather windows, and crew coordination require real project management. We provide a schedule before work starts and communicate changes proactively.

## Common Problems We See in Tinley Park Commercial Properties

**Parapet wall deterioration.** Parapet walls — the masonry above the roofline — are exposed to weather on both faces and the top. Cap joints at parapet tops are among the first to fail. Water entering through failed parapet joints causes roof membrane damage and interior infiltration simultaneously.

**Lintel failure at commercial openings.** Commercial buildings with many window and door openings often show lintel-line deterioration at multiple locations. When lintels rust and expand, the pattern of brick displacement above openings is distinctive. Multi-opening lintel work is a common commercial scope.

**Widespread mortar deterioration on aging buildings.** Buildings from the 1980s and 1990s built with Portland-heavy mortar mixes often develop accelerated joint failure as the mortar carbonates and loses adhesion to the brick. By the time the building is 30–35 years old, significant repointing scope is common.

**Previous improper repairs.** Caulking over open mortar joints, painting over stained brick, and surface patching without joint removal — all of these are common in commercial buildings where prior contractors cut corners. Removing failed repairs and doing the underlying work correctly is part of many commercial restoration scopes.
