## The Project

When a historic Chicagoland church noticed water intrusion and severely deteriorated mortar joints on their 80-year-old brick façade, they called Emerald Masonry for a full assessment. What we found was a classic case of deferred maintenance — decades of freeze-thaw cycles had opened mortar joints across the entire south and west elevations, creating a direct path for water into the building envelope.

## What We Found

Our on-site estimate identified three key problem areas:

- **South elevation:** Full repointing required — mortar had receded up to ¾" in places
- **Window surrounds:** Cracked and spalling mortar at every header
- **Parapet wall:** Open joints and deteriorated cap bricks allowing water to pool and penetrate

## Our Approach

We developed a dedicated project schedule for the church administration so they could plan around services and events. Work was staged in phases to minimize disruption to the congregation.

All failing mortar was ground out to a minimum depth of ¾" using angle grinders and oscillating tools. We matched the original mortar color and composition as closely as possible, preserving the historic character of the building while meeting current standards for water resistance.

## The Result

The full restoration took three weeks across two phases. The church now has a watertight building envelope, restored structural integrity at the parapet, and mortar joints that should last 20–30 years with routine maintenance.

The property manager noted that two previous contractors had patched over failing mortar without addressing the underlying deterioration. Our approach — grinding out the old material completely — is the only way to do it right.

## Why This Matters for Church Properties

Churches and religious institutions face unique challenges with masonry maintenance:

- Large building footprints mean more total linear footage of mortar joints
- Historic architecture requires matching materials and methods
- Budget cycles don't always align with maintenance needs
- Water intrusion can damage irreplaceable interior finishes

**Proactive tuckpointing every 20–25 years** is significantly less expensive than dealing with the structural damage and interior repairs that result from years of water intrusion.
